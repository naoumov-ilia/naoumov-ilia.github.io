## Introduction

Let us consider a model of price wave motion over time.

## Main section

Consider the chart of price as a function of time.

Let the price rise from its minimum value at point **o1** to point **v1**, then decline to point **f1**, after which growth continues.

Chart: [open diagram]({{ '/en/diagrams/graph-1/' | relative_url }})

### Definitions

- **(o1 → v1)** — impulse wave
- **(v1 → f1)** — correction (corrective wave)
- **(o1 → f1)** — wave motion

Duration:

`T1 = t(f1) - t(o1)`

where `(a:b)` denotes the price movement between points `a` and `b`.

### Basic properties

The following statements will be regarded as evident:

- from the start of the correction until its completion, the price at the starting point is maximal
- after the correction is completed, the price does not fall to the value at the endpoint
- the duration of the correction cannot exceed the duration of the preceding impulse wave

### Important clarification

In the general case, the beginning of the correction **v1** is not the point of the maximum price of the impulse wave.

> The statement is always valid when the maximum point occurs before the moment  
> `t = t(o1) + T1 / 2`

### Set of correction endpoints

A single correction starting point generally corresponds to a set of possible correction endpoints:

`{f1}`

This set includes all values, including values inside a bar, after each of which the price does not return to that level.

See illustration: [chart with f1 variants]({{ '/en/diagrams/graph-2/' | relative_url }})

## Conclusion

The model considered makes it possible to formalize the structure of price movement and identify the key elements of the wave process.
